The problem identified

Consumers should be able to know how much greenhouse gas emissions they are contributing to.
Image by: Unsplash

According to PNAS, approximately 98% of all published climate scientists support the consensus on climate change. Food production is the cause of one fourth of the planets CO₂ emissions. This includes all of the steps from initial production, transportation and packaging, before it ends up in your local store.

According to a study done by the WWF in Sweden called “One Planet Plate”, we have to adjust our weekly food intake to a sustainable level to not exceed 11kg CO₂-equivalents. If many people make small changes, the impact will be big. There are currently no commercial tool which helps you track your own CO₂ emissions, when it comes to grocery shopping. Therefore we conducted a survey to see if there is an interest among the common consumer for this service. Participants were asked if they agreed or disagreed to the following statement:


"I would like to know the amount
of emissions I produce"





We made an assumption that if the average consumer was made more aware what impact agricultural produce has on the environment, they would tend to go for a more environmentally friendly alternative.
A survey we conducted confirmed parts of our assumption. 78% of the participants answered that they want to know how much they personally are contributing to greenhouse gas emissions.

We identified that there are not enough services on the market that can aid consumers with easy access to information on the emissions of the grocery items that they choose to buy on a daily basis. If you are able to make educated decisions on the spot, you can take more control over your greenhouse gas emissions.
Therefore developed a platform where the average consumer can track their emissions when shopping groceries.
